### 1.3 Introduction Ï¥àÏïà (Revised)

---

**[Introduction Draft - ÏßÅÍ¥Ä Ï§ëÏã¨]**

**Hallucination** in Large Language Models (LLMs) occurs when a model generates content that sounds plausible but is factually incorrect. However, not all hallucinations are the same. We argue that there are fundamentally **two types of hallucination**, each requiring different detection approaches.

**Type A: Confusion among known possibilities.** When a model has learned multiple related pieces of information, it may confuse them. For instance, when asked about historical dates, a well-trained model might mix up similar events it has encountered during training. In this case, the model "knows" the relevant domain but makes errors due to interference.

**Type B: Confabulation about unknown topics.** When a model encounters a question about something rarely or never seen during training, it cannot retrieve relevant knowledge. Instead, it *fabricates* a plausible-sounding answer. The model doesn't "know" the answer‚Äîit invents one.

These two types of errors leave different traces in model behavior:

- **Type A (Confusion)**: The model produces *diverse* responses, reflecting its uncertainty among multiple learned possibilities. **Semantic Entropy** captures this diversity effectively.

- **Type B (Confabulation)**: The model produces *consistent* but fabricated responses, because it has only one invented story to tell. Semantic Entropy fails here (the "zero-entropy problem"). However, **Semantic Energy**‚Äîwhich measures raw model confidence‚Äîcan detect the subtle signs of fabrication.

**The key insight**: The type of hallucination depends on whether the model has learned the relevant knowledge. This, in turn, can be estimated from **pre-training corpus statistics**. If an entity or relationship appears frequently in the training corpus, the model likely "knows" it. If not, any answer is likely fabrication.

**Our contribution**: We propose a **corpus-aware adaptive approach** that:
1. Estimates whether the model has learned the relevant knowledge using corpus statistics
2. Applies **Semantic Entropy** when the model likely knows (to detect confusion)
3. Applies **Semantic Energy** when the model likely doesn't know (to detect confabulation)

This principled approach outperforms fixed-weight combinations and provides interpretable insights into *why* a particular hallucination occurred.

## 1.8 Formal Definitions (Addressing Clarity Concerns)

### 1.6.1 Corpus Coverage

**Definition**: Corpus coverage $C(q, a)$ for a question-answer pair $(q, a)$ quantifies the likelihood that the model has learned the relevant knowledge during pre-training.

$$C(q, a) = \alpha \cdot \text{FreqScore}(E_q \cup E_a) + \beta \cdot \text{CoocScore}(E_q, E_a)$$

where:
- $E_q$: entities extracted from question $q$
- $E_a$: entities extracted from answer $a$
- $\text{FreqScore}(E) = \frac{1}{|E|} \sum_{e \in E} \frac{\log(1 + \text{freq}(e))}{\log(1 + \text{freq}_{\max})}$
- $\text{CoocScore}(E_q, E_a) = \frac{1}{|E_q||E_a|} \sum_{e_q \in E_q} \sum_{e_a \in E_a} \mathbb{1}[\text{cooc}(e_q, e_a) > 0]$
- $\alpha + \beta = 1$ (hyperparameters, default: $\alpha = 0.5, \beta = 0.5$)

**Interpretation**:
- $C(q, a) \to 1$: High coverage ‚Üí model likely knows this knowledge
- $C(q, a) \to 0$: Low coverage ‚Üí model likely doesn't know this knowledge

### 1.6.2 Zero-Entropy Problem

**Definition**: The zero-entropy problem occurs when a model produces semantically identical responses across multiple samples, resulting in $SE \approx 0$, regardless of correctness.

$$\text{Zero-SE condition}: \quad |\{C_k\}| = 1 \implies SE = -1 \cdot \log(1) = 0$$

**Problem**: When $SE = 0$:
- If correct: Model confidently knows the answer ‚úì
- If incorrect: Model confidently fabricates the same wrong answer ‚úó

SE cannot distinguish these two cases. Energy can help by measuring internal confidence.

### 1.6.3 Adaptive Weighting Formula

**Definition**: The corpus-aware adaptive score combines SE and Energy based on corpus coverage:

$$\text{Score}(q, a) = w(C) \cdot \tilde{E}(a) + (1 - w(C)) \cdot \widetilde{SE}(a)$$

where:
- $\tilde{E}(a) = \frac{E(a) - E_{\min}}{E_{\max} - E_{\min}}$: normalized Energy (higher = more uncertain)
- $\widetilde{SE}(a) = \frac{SE(a)}{SE_{\max}}$: normalized Semantic Entropy
- $w(C)$: weight function based on corpus coverage $C$

**Weight function options**:

1. **Sigmoid** (smooth transition):
$$w(C) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(k \cdot (C - \mu))}$$

2. **Linear** (simple):
$$w(C) = w_{\max} - (w_{\max} - w_{\min}) \cdot C$$

3. **Threshold** (discrete):
$$w(C) = \begin{cases} 0.9 & \text{if } C < \tau_1 \\ 0.5 & \text{if } \tau_1 \leq C < \tau_2 \\ 0.1 & \text{if } C \geq \tau_2 \end{cases}$$

### 1.6.4 Theoretical Foundation: Why Corpus Statistics Work

**Claim**: Corpus frequency is a valid proxy for model knowledge because:

1. **Training objective**: LLMs minimize next-token prediction loss. Frequent patterns receive more gradient updates ‚Üí better learned.

2. **Empirical evidence** (from QuCo-RAG, Min et al. 2025):
   - Low-frequency entities correlate with higher hallucination rates
   - Zero co-occurrence strongly predicts fabricated relationships
   - Cross-model transfer works: OLMo corpus statistics apply to Llama, Qwen, GPT

3. **Information-theoretic intuition**:
   - High frequency ‚Üí low surprise ‚Üí low perplexity ‚Üí model "knows"
   - Low frequency ‚Üí high surprise ‚Üí high perplexity ‚Üí model "guesses"

**Connection to SE/Energy effectiveness**:

| Corpus State | Model State | Error Type | Behavioral Signal | Best Detector |
|--------------|-------------|------------|-------------------|---------------|
| High coverage | Knows | Confusion | Diverse responses | SE |
| Low coverage | Doesn't know | Confabulation | Consistent fabrication | Energy |
